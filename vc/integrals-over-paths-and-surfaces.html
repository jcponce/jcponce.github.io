Integrals Over Paths and Surfaces

The Path Integral

Suppose we have a scalar function $f: \R^3 \to \R,$ so that $f$ sends points in $\R^3$ to real numbers. It will be useful to define the integral of such functions $f$ along a path \[ \c : [a,b]\to \R^3, \text{ where } \c(t) = \left(x(t), y(t), z(t) \right). \]

If $\c:[a,b]\to \R^3$ is of class $C^1$ and the composite function $t\mapsto f \left(x(t), y(t), z(t) \right)$ is continuous on $[a,b],$ then the path integral, or the integral of $f(x,y,z)$ along the path $\c,$ is defined as \[ \int_{\c} f\,ds = \int_a^b f \left(x(t), y(t), z(t) \right)\norm{\c'(t)}\, dt. \]

Sometimes $\int_{\c}f\,ds$ is denoted as \[ \int_{\c}f(x,y,z)\,ds \] or \[ \int_{a}^{b} f\left(\c(t)\right)\norm{\c'(t)}\, dt. \] If $\c$ is only piecewise $C^1$, or $f\left(\c(t)\right)$ is piecewise continuous, we define $\int_{\c}f\,ds$ by breaking $[a,b]$ into pieces over which $f\left(\c(t)\right)\norm{\c'(t)}$ is continuous, and then we sum the integrals over the pieces.

Let $\c$ be the helix $\c:[0,2\pi]\to \R^3$ define as $t\mapsto \left(\cos t, \sin t, t\right),$ and let $f(x,y,z)=x^2+ y^2+ z^2.$

To compute $\int_{\c}f(x,y,z)\,ds,$ first we need $\norm{\c'(t)}:$ \begin{eqnarray*} \norm{\c'(t)} &=& \sqrt{ \left[\frac{d(\cos t)}{dt}\right]^2 + \left[\frac{d(\sin t)}{dt}\right]^2+ \left[\frac{dt}{dt}\right]^2}\\ &=& \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + 1} \\ &=& \sqrt{2}. \end{eqnarray*}

Next we substitute for $x,y,z$ in terms of $t$ to obtain \begin{eqnarray*} f(x,y,z) &=& x^2+y^2+z^2\\ &=& \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + t^2}\\ &=& 1+ t^2. \end{eqnarray*} along the path $\c.$ Thus we obtain \begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c}f(x,y,z)\,ds &=& \int_0^{2pi} \left(1+ t^2\right)\sqrt{2}\,dt\\ &=& \sqrt{2} \left[\frac{t^3}{3}+ t\right]_0^{2\pi}\\ &=& \frac{2\sqrt{2}\pi}{3} \left(4\pi^2 + 3\right). \end{eqnarray*}

The definition of path integral can be understood more intuitively by approximating the path integral using sums that resemble Riemann sums in calculus. For simplicity, let $\c$ be of $C^1$ on $I=[a,b].$ Subdivide the interval $I=[a,b]$ by means of a partition \[ a=t_0 \lt t_1 \lt \ldots \lt t_N=b. \] Now $\c$ divided into smaller paths $\c_i$ defined on $\left[t_i,t_{i+1}\right]$ for $0\le i \le N-1.$

We consider now the arc length $\Delta s_i$ of $\c_i$ defined as \[ \Delta s_i = \int_{t_i}^{t_{i+1}}\norm{\c'(t)}\, dt. \]

If $N$ is large, the arc length $\Delta s_i$ is small and $f(x,y,z)$ is approximately constant for points on $\c_i.$ Now we consider the sums \[ S_N = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1}f\left(x_i, y_i, z_i\right)\delta s_i, \] where $\left(x_i, y_i, z_i\right)= \c(t)$ for some $t_i\in \left[t_i,t_{i+1}\right].$ By the mean-value theorem we know that $\Delta s_i=\norm{\c'(t_i^{*})}\Delta t_i,$ where $t_i^*\in \left[t_i,t_{i+1}\right]$ and $\Delta t_i = t_{i+1}-t_i.$ Thus, from calculus, we have \begin{eqnarray*} \lim_{N\to \infty} S_N & = & \lim_{N\to \infty} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1}f\left(x_i, y_i, z_i\right) \norm{\c'(t_i^{*})}\Delta t_i \\ & = &\int_{I}f\left(x(t),y(t),z(t)\right)\norm{c'(t)}\,dt \\ & = & \int_{\c} f\left(x,y,z\right)~ds. \end{eqnarray*}

Suppose that image of $\c$ represents a wire. If $f(x,y,z)$ represents the mass density at $(x,y,z),$ then the integral of $f$ will be the total mass of the wire. On the other hand, if $f(x,y,z)$ indicates temperature, then the integral of $f$ determines the average temperature along the wire.

The Path Integral for Planar Curves

A particular case of the path integral happens when the path $\c$ describes a plane curve. Assume that all points $\c(t)$ lie in the $xy$ plane and $f$ is a real-valued function of two variables. The path integral of $f$ along $\c$ is

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c} f(x,y)~ ds = \int_a^b f\left(x(t),y(t)\right)\sqrt{\left[x'(t)\right]^2+\left[y'(t)\right]^2}~dt. \end{eqnarray*}

If $f(x,y)\geq 0,$ this integral has a geometric interpretation as the area of a fence. It is possible to construct a "fence" with base the image of $\c$ and with height $f(x,y)$ at $(x,y).$ If $\c$ moves only once along the image of $\c,$ then $\int_{\c}f(x,y)\,ds$ represents the area of a side of this fence.

Line Integrals

Work done by force fields

Consider a force field $\F(x,y)$ and a piecewiese continuous smooth curve $C.$ We wish to compute the work done by this force in moving a particle along a smooth curve $C.$

We divide $C$ into subarcs $P_{i-1}$-$P_i$ with lengths $\Delta s_i$ by dividing the parameter interval $[a, b]$ into subintervals of equal width.

Choose a point $P_i^*$ on the $i$th subarc corresponding to the parameter value $t_i^*.$ If $\Delta s_i$ is small, then as the particle moves from $P_{i-1}$ to $P_i$ along the curve, it proceeds approximately in the direction of $\T(t_i^*),$ the unit tangent vector at $P_i^*.$

Thus the work done by the force $\F$ in moving the particle from $P_{i-1}$ to $P_i$ is approximately \[ \F^* \pd \T^* \Delta s_i \]

Thus the work done by the force $\F$ in moving the particle from $P_{i-1}$ to $P_i$ is approximately \[ \F^* \pd \T^* \Delta s_i \]

and the total work done in moving the particle along $C$ is approximately \[ \sum_{i=1}^n \F^* \pd \T^* \Delta s_i \]

Intuitively, we see that these approximations have to become better as $n\to \infty$ ($\Delta s_i\to 0$).

Therefore we define the work $W$ done by the force field $\F$ as the limit of the Riemann sums in $\sum_{i=1}^n \F^* \pd \T^* \Delta s_i.$ That is \[ W = \int_C\F\pd \T~ds \]

The previous discussion of work in two dimensions motivates the following definition.
Let $\F$ be a vector field on $\R^3$ that is continuous on the path $\c':[a,b]\to \R^3.$ The line integral of $\F$ along $\c$ is defined as \[ \int_{\c} \F\pd d\s = \int_a^b \F\left(\c(t)\right)\pd \c'(t)~dt. \] In other words, we integrate the dot product of $\F$ with $\c'$ over the interval $[a,b].$

Similarly to the case with scalar functions, we can also define $ \int_{\c} \F\pd d\s $ when $ \F\left(\c(t)\right)\pd \c'(t)$ is only piecewise continuous.

Common notation

Let $\,\F= F_1(x,y,z)~\i + F_2(x,y,z)~\j + F_3(x,y,z)~\k$ be a vector field. If the paths $\c$ satisfy $\c'(t)\neq \mathbf 0,$ the line integral $\int_{\c} \F \pd d\s$ is often expressed as \[ \int_{\c} \F \pd \T ~ds, \] where $\T = \c'(t)/\norm{\c'(t)}$ denotes the tangle unit vector.

To prove this let $\c(t) =x(t)~\i+y(t)~\j+z(t)~\k$ with $t\in[a,b].$ Then the unit tangent vector to $\c$ is

\[ \T=\frac{\c'(t)}{\norm{\c'(t)}} \quad \text{and} \quad ds = \norm{\c'(t)}dt. \]

Thus

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c} \F\pd \T ~ds &=& \int_a^b \left[\F(\c(t)) \pd \frac{\c'(t)}{\norm{\c'(t)}}\right]\norm{\c'(t)}dt \\ &=& \int_a^b \F(\c(t)) \pd \c'(t)~dt \\ &=& \int_{\c} \F \pd d\s. \end{eqnarray*}

Another common way to write $\int_{\c} \F \pd d\s$ is \[ \int_{\c}F_1~dx+ F_2~dy + F_3~dz, \] where $F_1,$ $F_2$ and $F_3$ are the components of the vector field $\F.$ The expression $F_1~dx+ F_2~dy + F_3~dz$ is called a differential form. By definition, the integral of a differential form along a path $\c(t) = x(t)~\i+y(t)~\j+z(t)~\k$ is

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c}F_1~dx+ F_2~dy + F_3~dz &=& \int_{\c}\left(F_1\frac{dx}{dt}+F_2\frac{dy}{dt}+F_3\frac{dz}{dt}\right)dt\\ &=& \int_{\c}\F \pd d\s. \end{eqnarray*}

In this case we consider $d\s$ as the differential form $d\s = dx~\i+dy~\j+dz~\k.$ This is why the differential form $F_1~dx+ F_2~dy + F_3~dz$ can be written as the dot product $\F\pd d\s.$

It is also common to write $\int_{\c} \F \pd d\s$ using the notation \[ \int_C \F \pd d\r. \] The main reason is that we can think of describing a $C^1$ path $\c$ in terms of a moving position vector based at the origin and ending at the point $\c(t)$ at time $t.$ Postion vectors are ofter denoted by \[ \r = x~\j+y~\j+z~\k, \] and so the curve $C$ is described using the notation \[ \r(t) = x(t)~\j+y(t)~\j+z(t)~\k \] instead of $\c(t).$ By definition, the line integral is then given by \[ \int_a^b \F \big(\r(t)\big)\pd \frac{d\r}{dr}dt. \] By canceling the $dt$'s, and using the parametrization independence to replace the limits of integration with the geometric curve $C,$ we obtain the notation $\int_C \F \pd d\r.$

To compute a line integral in any particular case, we can either use the original definition or integrate the tangencial component of $\F$ along $\c,$ whichever is easier or more appropiate.

Let $c(t) = \left(\sin t, \cos t , t\right)$ with $t\in [-, 2\pi],$ and $\F(x,y,z) = x~\i + y~\j + z~\k.$ Here we have that \[ c'(t) = \left(\cos t, -\sin t , 1\right). \] Then \begin{eqnarray*} \F(\c(t))\pd \c'(t) &=& \left(\sin t, \cos t, t\right) \pd \left(\cos t, -\sin t , t\right)\\ &=& \sin t\cos t -\cos t \sin t+ t = t, \end{eqnarray*} and so \[ \int_{\c}\F\pd d\s = \int_0^{2\pi} t~dt = 2\pi^2. \]
To evaluate \[ \int_{\c} \cos z~dx + e^x ~dy + e^y~dz \] where $\c$ is the path defined as $\c(t) = \i + t~\j + e^t~\k,$ with $t\in [0,2],$ we start by computing
\[ \frac{dx}{dt}=0,\quad \frac{dy}{dt}=1,\quad \frac{dz}{dt}=e^t. \]

Therefore

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c} F_1~dx+ F_2~dy + F_3~dz &=& \int_0^2 \left(0+e+e^{2t}\right)dt \\ &=& \left[et + \frac{1}{2} e^{2t}\right]_0^2\\ &=& 2e + \frac{1}{2}e^4 - \frac{1}{2}. \end{eqnarray*}
Suppose $\F$ is the force vector field $F(x,y,z) = x^3~\i + y~\j + z~\k.$ A circle of radius $R$ in the $yz$ plane can be represented with the parametrization \[ \c(\theta) = \left(0, R \cos \theta, R \sin \theta \right) \] with $0\le \theta \le 2\pi.$

Since $\F\left(\c (\theta)\right)\pd \c'(\theta)= 0,$ the force field $\F$ is normal to the circle at every point on the circle, which means that $F$ will not do any work on a particle moving along the circle.

We can easily verify this by computing:

\begin{eqnarray*} W &=& \int_{\c}\F \pd d\s = \int_{\c}x^3~dx + y~dy + z~dz\\ &=& \int_0^{2\pi} \left(0 - R^2\cos \theta \sin \theta + R^2 \cos \theta \sin\theta \right)d\theta = 0. \end{eqnarray*}

If we consider the path $\c(t) = \cos^3 t~\i + \sin^3 t~\j +t~\k$ for $t\in[0, 7\pi/2],$ then the work done by the field is $-1/2$ (Compute the integral to confirm this). This means that the field impedes movement along the path.

Line Integrals of Gradient Fields

Recall that a vector field $\F$ is a gradient vector field if $\F= \nabla f$ for some real-valued function $f.$ Thus \[ \F = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}~\i+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}~\j+\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}~\k. \]

From calculus we also learned that if $f$ and $F$ are single-valued continuous functions defined on a closed interval $[a,b],$ and also $F$ is differentiable on $(a,b),$ such that $F'=f;$ then \[ \int_a^bf(x)~dx = F(b)-F(a), \] by the fundamenal theorem of calculus. Thus the value of the integral of $f$ depends only on the value of $F$ at the end points of the interval $[a,b].$

If a vector field $\F$ is the derivative of a function $f$, is it possible to determine the value of $\int_{\c}\F\pd d\s$ by the value of $f$ at the endpoints $\c(a)$ and $\c(b)?$ The answer is contained in the following gereralization of the fundamental theorem of calculus.

Suppose $f:\R^3 \to \R$ is of class $C^1$ and that $\c:[a,b]\to R^3$ is a piecewise $C^1$ path. Then \[ \int_{\c}\nabla f \pd d\s = f\big(\c(b)\big) -f\big(\c(a)\big). \]
Consider the function $F(t)= \big(f\circ \c\big)(t) = f\big(\c(t)\big) $ for $t\in[a,b].$ Then \[ F'(t) = \nabla f \big(\c(t)\big) \pd \c'(t). \] Since $F$ is a real-valued function on the variable $t$, by the fundamental theorem of calculus of single-variable calculus,
\[ \int_a^b F'(t)~dt = F(b)-F(a) = f\big(\c(b)\big) -f\big(\c(a)\big) \]

Hence

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_a^b \nabla f \pd d\s &=& \int_a^b \nabla f \big(\c(t)\big) \pd \c'(t)\\ &=& \int_a^b F'(t)~dt \\ &=& F(b)-F(a) \\ &=& f\big(\c(b)\big) -f\big(\c(a)\big) \end{eqnarray*}
Let $\c(t)= \left(t^4/4, \sin^3(t\pi / 2), 0\right)$ with $t\in[0,1].$ We would like to evaluate \[ \int_{\c}y ~dx + x~dy. \] First note that from the expression $y ~dx + x~dy,$ we can deduce that the vector field is $\F = y~\i + x~\j + 0~\k,$ which has a gradient $f(x,y,z)=xy.$ Thus \[ f\big(\c(t)\big)=\left(\frac{t^4}{4}\right)\cdot \sin\left(\frac{t\pi}{2}\right). \] Therefore
\begin{eqnarray*} \int_{\c}y ~dx + x~dy &=& f\big(\c(1)\big) -f\big(\c(0)\big)\\ &=& \frac{1}{4}(1) - 0 = \frac{1}{4}. \end{eqnarray*}

Parametrization of surfaces

Another way of representing a surface $S$ in $\R^3$ is by a parametrization. This is where the coordinate variables are functions of two parameters $u$ and $v$: $$ x=x(u,v),\ \ y=y(u,v),\ \ z=z(u,v) $$ and the vector $$ \PH(u,v) = x(u,v)~\i+y(u,v)~\j+z(u,v)~\k $$ traces out the surface as $u,v$ vary over some region $D$ in the "$uv$-plane". So for every point $(u,v)$ in $D,$ there corresponds a point on the surface $S$.

The following diagram shows the point $P$ on the surface $S$ which corresponds to the point $(u,v)$ in the region $D$ in the $uv$-plane. As $(u,v)$ moves around all points in $D,$ the point $P$ moves around in $S,$ tracing out the entire surface.

Note that a surface defined explicitly by $z=f(x,y)$ is equivalent to a parametrization $$ \r(x,y) = x~\i+y~\j+f(x,y)~\k, $$ where we treat the coordinate variables $x$ and $y$ as the parameters. Note that we have not specified any bounds on the variables. Often the challenge is to not only find suitable functions for a parametrization, but for a finite surface to determine bounds on the parameters.

What's the point to define parametrizations of surfaces?

A good motivation to use parametrizations is that sometimes surfaces cannot be the graphs of functions defined as \[ z= f(x,y). \]

Another reason is given by the limitations of the computer considering the expression $z= f(x,y).$ For example if we try to plot \[ x^2+y^2+z^2=4. \]

The Derivative

Definition: Let $I$ be an interval, let $f \colon I \to \R$ be a function, and let $c \in I$. If the limit \begin{equation*} L := \lim_{x \to c} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \end{equation*} exists, then we say $f$ is differentiable at $c$, that $L$ is the derivative of $f$ at $c$, and write $f'(c) := L$.

If $f$ is differentiable at all $c \in I$, then we simply say that $f$ is differentiable, and then we obtain a function $f' \colon I \to \R$.

The expression $\displaystyle\frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}$ is called the difference quotient.

The Derivative

Theorem: Let $I$ be an interval, let $f \colon I \to \R$ and $g \colon I \to \R$ be differentiable at $c \in I$, and let $\alpha \in \R$.

  1. Define $h \colon I \to \R$ by $h(x) := \alpha f(x)$. Then $h$ is differentiable at $c$ and \[h'(c) = \alpha f'(c).\]
  2. Define $h \colon I \to \R$ by $h(x) := f(x) + g(x)$. Then $h$ is differentiable at $c$ and \[h'(c) = f'(c) + g'(c).\]

Product rule

Theorem: Let $I$ be an interval, let $f \colon I \to \R$ and $g \colon I \to \R$ be functions differentiable at $c$. If $h \colon I \to \R$ is defined by \begin{equation*} h(x) := f(x) g(x) , \end{equation*} then $h$ is differentiable at $c$ and \begin{equation*} h'(c) = f(c) g'(c) + f'(c) g(c) . \end{equation*}

The Derivative

Quotient rule

Theorem: Let $I$ be an interval, let $f \colon I \to \R$ and $g \colon I \to \R$ be differentiable at $c$ and $g(x) \not= 0$ for all $x \in I$. If $h \colon I \to \R$ is defined by \begin{equation*} h(x) := \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}, \end{equation*} then $h$ is differentiable at $c$ and \begin{equation*} h'(c) = \frac{f'(c) g(c) - f(c) g'(c)}{{\bigl(g(c)\bigr)}^2} . \end{equation*}

Chain rule

Theorem: Let $I_1, I_2$ be intervals, let $g \colon I_1 \to I_2$ be differentiable at $c \in I_1$, and $f \colon I_2 \to \R$ be differentiable at $g(c)$. If $h \colon I_1 \to \R$ is defined by \begin{equation*} h(x) := (f \circ g) (x) = f\bigl(g(x)\bigr) , \end{equation*} then $h$ is differentiable at $c$ and \begin{equation*} h'(c) = f'\bigl(g(c)\bigr)g'(c) . \end{equation*}

Rolle's Theorem

Theorem: Let $f \colon [a,b] \to \R$ be continuous function differentiable on $(a,b)$ such that $f(a) = f(b)$. Then there exists a $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f'(c) = 0$.

Mean Value Theorem

Theorem: Let $f \colon [a,b] \to \R$ be a continuous function differentiable on $(a,b)$. Then there exists a point $c \in (a,b)$ such that \begin{equation*} f(b)-f(a) = f'(c)(b-a) . \end{equation*}

  2. $f$ is decreasing if and only if $f'(x) \leq 0$ for all $x \in I$.

Taylor polynomial

Definition: For an $n$ times differentiable function $f$ defined near a point $x_0 \in \R$, define the $n$th order Taylor polynomial for $f$ at $x_0$ as \begin{equation*} {\small \begin{split} P_n^{x_0}(x) & := \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(x_0)}{k!}{(x-x_0)}^k \\ & = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{f''(x_0)}{2}{(x-x_0)}^2 + \cdots + \frac{f^{(n)}(x_0)}{n!}{(x-x_0)}^n . \end{split} } \end{equation*}

Taylor's Theorem

Theorem: Suppose $f \colon [a,b] \to \R$ is a function with $n$ continuous derivatives on $[a,b]$ and such that $f^{(n+1)}$ exists on $(a,b)$. Given distinct points $x_0$ and $x$ in $[a,b]$, we can find a point $c$ between $x_0$ and $x$ such that \begin{equation*} f(x)=P_{n}^{x_0}(x)+\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}{(x-x_0)}^{n+1} . \end{equation*}

Inverse function Theorem

Theorem: Let $f \colon (a,b) \to \R$ be a continuously differentiable function, $x_0 \in (a,b)$ a point where $f'(x_0) \not= 0$. Then there exists an open interval $I \subset (a,b)$ with $x_0 \in I$, the restriction $f|_{I}$ is injective with a continuously differentiable inverse $g \colon J \to I$ defined on an interval $J := f(I)$, and \begin{equation*} g'(y) = \frac{1}{f'\bigl( g(y) \bigr)} \qquad \text{for all } y \in J. \end{equation*}
